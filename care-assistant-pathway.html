What Does a Care Assistant Do?

Care assistants (Pflegefachhelfer or Betreuungskraft) provide hands-on support to elderly and disabled people in care homes, hospitals, and home settings.

Daily Responsibilities

  • Help clients with personal hygiene (bathing, dressing, toileting)
  • Prepare and serve meals
  • Provide emotional support and companionship
  • Monitor health and report changes to nurses
  • Keep living spaces clean and safe
  • Assist with mobility and movement
  • Accompany clients to appointments or activities

Work Environment

  • Care homes (Pflegeheime): Elderly care facilities, structured shifts
  • Hospitals: Support roles in geriatric wards, medical facilities
  • Home care (ambulante Pflege): Visit clients in their homes, flexible scheduling
  • Assisted living facilities: Help people maintain independence at home

Required Skills & Traits

  • Compassion and patience with elderly people
  • Physical stamina (standing, lifting, walking 6–8 hours daily)
  • Basic German language ability (B1)
  • Reliability and professional attitude
  • Basic hygiene and safety knowledge

How to Qualify as a Care Assistant

Unlike nursing, care assistant work has multiple pathways with different training requirements. Choose the fastest route for your background.

Option 1: Quick-Start Program (3–6 months)

Many employers offer paid training programs for care assistants. This is the fastest path.

  • Structure: On-the-job training at a care facility + classroom instruction
  • Duration: 3–6 months (depends on prior experience)
  • Cost: FREE — Employer pays (you earn while training)
  • Requirements: German B1 level, job offer from care facility
  • Result: Certificate as care assistant (Pflegefachhelfer)

Option 2: Formal 2-Year Program

If you want advancement to nursing or higher responsibility roles.

  • Structure: Berufsausbildung (vocational training) pathway
  • Duration: 2–3 years with work-study format
  • Salary: €650–€1,100/month during training
  • Requirements: Secondary school diploma, German B1
  • Result: Certified care specialist (Pflegefachfrau/Pflegefachmann)

Option 3: Upgrade Path (For existing carers)

If you have care experience in your home country.

  • Recognition assessment: Your prior experience may count
  • Gap training: 2–4 weeks to learn German healthcare standards
  • Start immediately: Begin work while completing formal certification
  • Employer support: Many employers fund your training

Fastest Timeline to Start Work

3–4 months: Learn German (B1), secure job offer, complete employer training, start working.

How to Get a Care Assistant Job

The job market for care assistants is tight — employers are eager to hire. Your main job is learning German and positioning yourself.

Step 1: Prepare Your German (4–8 months)

  • Target: B1 German level (working level)
  • Cost: €500–€1,500
  • Timeline: 6–8 months of consistent study
  • Exam: Goethe B1 or similar (€150–€200)

Step 2: Build Your Application

  • German CV highlighting any care experience
  • Motivation letter (why you want care work)
  • Certificates/references from previous care work
  • Health clearance (TB test, basic medical check)

Step 3: Find Care Facility Positions

  • Use job sites: Indeed.de, StepStone.de, Careerjet.de
  • Direct approach: Call care homes and ask about openings
  • Recruitment agencies: Specialize in care worker placement
  • We connect you with facilities in our network

Step 4: Apply & Interview

  • Apply to 15–20 positions
  • Interviews typically via phone/video
  • Test your German language skills
  • Discuss your care experience and availability

Step 5: Job Offer & Visa

  • Once you have job offer, apply for work visa
  • Processing: 4–6 weeks
  • Cost: €100 (visa fee — employer often covers)
  • You can often start before visa arrives

Timeline from Decision to Starting Work

5–8 months: German training (4–6 months) + job search + visa (4–6 weeks).
